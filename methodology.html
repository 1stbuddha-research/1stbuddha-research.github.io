1. Microscopic Surface Study

Every Nepal bronze is examined under a stereo microscope. The inspection focuses on: engraved robe patterns, tool marks, mineral deposits, copper oxidation, cuprite layers, malachite micro-crystallisation and remains of original fire gilding. These results form the backbone of the technical analysis.

2. UV-Light Examination

UV-light reveals organic residues, pigment remains and surface treatments not visible under normal lighting. Fire-gilded gold often shows characteristic fluorescence patterns. Modern coatings, adhesives or retouches normally fluoresce differently, making UV a reliable way to detect interventions.

3. Internal Casting Structures

Internal cavities are examined with endoscopic cameras. Early Nepal bronzes often show coarse, irregular sand-casting textures, mineral accretions, and structural oxidation that develops only over centuries. Modern casts typically present smoother, rotary-tool interiors or uniform walls.

4. Alloy Behaviour and Patina

The alloy composition influences patina formation. Copper-rich Nepal bronzes show deep red cuprite layers under green malachite. Surface polishing, grinding or machine finishing is documented and compared with expected historic patterns.

5. Comparative Style Analysis

After technical study, stylistic comparison is made with museum examples from the Kathmandu Valley, Mustang, Ladakh and Tibet. Engraving depth, robe patterning, lotus base construction and anatomical modelling confirm chronological placement.
